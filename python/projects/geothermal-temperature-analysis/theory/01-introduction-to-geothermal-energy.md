## 1. What is Geothermal Energy?

Geothermal energy is thermal energy stored within Earth's interior, continuously flowing outward toward the surface. The word comes from the Greek *geo* (Earth) and *therme* (heat).

Unlike solar or wind energy, geothermal energy is **not dependent on weather or time of day** — it is available 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, making it one of the most reliable renewable energy sources.

**Key physical definition:**

$$Q = -\lambda \frac{dT}{dz} \cdot A$$

Where:
- $Q$ = heat flow (W)
- $\lambda$ = thermal conductivity of rock (W/m·K)
- $dT/dz$ = geothermal gradient (°C/m)
- $A$ = cross-sectional area (m²)

The negative sign reflects the direction of heat flow: from hotter (deeper) to cooler (shallower) regions.

**Global average surface heat flux:** ~65 mW/m²  
**Total heat flow from Earth:** ~4.4 × 10¹³ W (44 terawatts)

This is roughly **2.5 times** the total global electricity consumption.

## 2. Why is the Earth Hot?

Earth's internal heat comes from **two primary sources**, with a third contributing minor amounts:

### 2.1 Primordial Heat (~50% of total)

When Earth formed ~4.5 billion years ago, accretion of planetesimals and the subsequent differentiation (separation of iron core from silicate mantle) released enormous gravitational potential energy. This heat is still escaping today.

- Accretion energy released: ~2.5 × 10³¹ J
- Core formation (differentiation) energy: ~1.5 × 10³¹ J

### 2.2 Radiogenic Heat (~50% of total)

Radioactive decay of long-lived isotopes in Earth's crust and mantle continuously generates heat:

| Isotope | Half-life | Heat production |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| ²³⁸U (Uranium-238) | 4.47 Ga | 9.46 × 10⁻⁵ W/kg |
| ²³²Th (Thorium-232) | 14.0 Ga | 2.64 × 10⁻⁵ W/kg |
| ⁴⁰K (Potassium-40) | 1.25 Ga | 2.92 × 10⁻⁵ W/kg |

These isotopes are concentrated in the **continental crust**, which is why continental geothermal gradients can differ significantly from oceanic ones.

### 2.3 Tidal Heating (minor, <0.1%)

Gravitational interactions with the Moon and Sun cause slight deformation of Earth, generating frictional heat. This effect is minor for Earth (unlike for Jupiter's moon Io).

### Temperature Profile of Earth

| Layer | Depth | Temperature |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| Crust (upper) | 0–35 km | 0–~1,000°C |
| Upper mantle | 35–400 km | ~1,000–1,500°C |
| Lower mantle | 400–2,890 km | ~1,500–3,700°C |
| Outer core (liquid Fe-Ni) | 2,890–5,150 km | ~3,700–5,000°C |
| Inner core (solid Fe-Ni) | 5,150–6,371 km | ~5,000–6,000°C |

## 3. How Geothermal Energy Works

Geothermal energy extraction requires three components: **heat source, fluid, and permeability**.

### 3.1 The Geothermal Gradient

The rate of temperature increase with depth is called the **geothermal gradient**:

$$\Gamma = \frac{dT}{dz}$$

- **Global average:** ~25–30°C/km
- **Volcanic/tectonically active regions:** 50–150°C/km (including most of Indonesia)
- **Old cratons (stable continental interiors):** ~10–15°C/km

This means drilling 3 km in an average geothermal area reaches ~90°C — sufficient for district heating. In Indonesia's volcanic areas, the same depth can reach 150–300°C.

### 3.2 Heat Transport Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Dominant in |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Conduction | Diffusive transfer through solid rock | Stable crust |
| Convection | Fluid circulation carrying heat | Hydrothermal systems |
| Advection | Heat transport by moving fluids | Fault zones, upwelling |
| Radiation | EM radiation | Negligible at crustal scale |

Most exploitable geothermal systems are **convection-dominated hydrothermal systems**, where meteoric water (rainwater) infiltrates deep, heats up, and rises back toward the surface.

### 3.3 The Conceptual Geothermal System Model

A conventional geothermal system has three zones:

```
Surface ─────────────────────────────────
         ↓ Recharge (cold meteoric water)
Cap Rock ─────────────────────────────────
   (low permeability, sealing layer)
Reservoir ────────────────────────────────
   (permeable rock saturated with
    hot fluid: ~150–300°C)
Heat Source ──────────────────────────────
   (magma body or anomalously
    hot basement rock)
```

## 4. Types of Geothermal Resources

### 4.1 Classification by Temperature

| Category | Temperature | Typical Use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| High-enthalpy | > 150°C | Electricity generation |
| Medium-enthalpy | 90–150°C | Binary power plants, industrial |
| Low-enthalpy | < 90°C | Direct use: heating, agriculture |

### 4.2 Classification by Geological Setting

**Volcanic/Magmatic Systems**
Associated with active volcanism. Temperatures 200–350°C at 1–3 km depth. Indonesia's primary resource type. Examples: Kamojang, Wayang Windu, Lahendong.

**Sedimentary Basin Systems**
Hot aquifers in deep sedimentary formations. Moderate temperatures (60–120°C) at 2–4 km depth. Common in Paris Basin (France), Pannonian Basin (Hungary).

**Hot Dry Rock / Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)**
No natural fluid or permeability. Water is injected, heated by hot rock, and extracted. The frontier of geothermal technology — potentially available anywhere.

**Geopressured Systems**
Deeply buried brines under abnormally high pressure. Combined thermal + pressure + methane energy. Mostly experimental.

### 4.3 Classification by Fluid Phase

| Type | Condition | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Vapor-dominated | Steam > liquid | High energy, rarer (e.g., The Geysers, USA; Kamojang, Indonesia) |
| Liquid-dominated | Liquid > steam | More common, requires flash separation |
| Two-phase | Both present | Mixed systems |

## 5. Applications of Geothermal Energy

### 5.1 Electricity Generation

Three main power plant technologies:

**Dry Steam Plants**
- Direct use of geothermal steam to drive turbine
- Requires vapor-dominated reservoir
- Oldest technology (Larderello, Italy, 1904)
- Efficiency: ~15–20%

**Flash Steam Plants** (most common worldwide)
- High-pressure hot fluid is "flashed" to steam in a separator
- Works with liquid-dominated reservoirs > 180°C
- Single flash (1 separator) or double flash (2 separators)

**Binary Cycle Plants**
- Geothermal fluid heats a secondary working fluid (isobutane, pentane) with lower boiling point
- Working fluid drives the turbine — no direct contact with geothermal fluid
- Works at lower temperatures (90–180°C)
- Closed loop: zero direct emissions
- Fastest growing technology globally

### 5.2 Direct Use Applications

Arranged by the **Lindal Diagram** (by temperature):

| Temperature | Application |
|-------------|-------------|
| > 150°C | Electricity, industrial drying |
| 100–150°C | Pasteurization, evaporation |
| 70–100°C | Space heating, greenhouse heating |
| 50–70°C | Aquaculture, soil warming |
| 20–50°C | Bathing (balneology), heat pumps |

### 5.3 Geothermal Heat Pumps (GHP)

GHPs use the stable shallow-ground temperature (10–15°C globally) as a heat exchange medium. They are not traditional geothermal energy but use the same subsurface thermal stability. COP (coefficient of performance) = 3–5 (3–5 units of heat per 1 unit of electricity used).

## 6. Advantages and Challenges

### 6.1 Advantages

| Advantage | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Baseload capacity | Capacity factor 90–95% vs ~25% solar, ~35% wind |
| Small land footprint | ~45 km² per TWh/year vs ~75 km² solar, ~72 km² wind |
| Low lifecycle emissions | 15–55 g CO₂eq/kWh vs ~820 coal, ~490 gas |
| Long lifespan | Power plants operating 30–50+ years |
| Local energy independence | Reduces fuel import dependence |
| Scalable | From kW (homes) to GW (national grid) |

### 6.2 Challenges

| Challenge | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Location-specific | Best resources concentrated in volcanic/tectonic zones |
| High upfront drilling cost | Exploration wells: USD 3–10 million each |
| Exploration risk | ~30–50% chance a drilled well is not commercially viable |
| Induced seismicity | Fluid injection/extraction can trigger microearthquakes |
| Reservoir decline | Overextraction can deplete pressure and temperature |
| H₂S emissions | Hydrogen sulfide in steam requires scrubbing |

## 7. Geothermal Energy in Indonesia

Indonesia sits on the **Pacific Ring of Fire**, where the Indo-Australian, Eurasian, and Pacific plates converge. This tectonic setting produces the world's highest concentration of active volcanoes (~130) and the largest geothermal potential of any country.

### 7.1 Resource Statistics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total geothermal potential | ~23.7 GW (40% of world total) |
| Installed capacity (2024) | ~2.4 GW |
| % of national electricity | ~5% |
| Government target 2030 | 7.2 GW |
| Number of identified areas | 312 locations |

### 7.2 Major Operating Fields

| Field | Province | Capacity | Type |
|-------|----------|----------|------|
| Kamojang | West Java | 235 MW | Vapor-dominated |
| Wayang Windu | West Java | 227 MW | Liquid-dominated |
| Salak (Darajat) | West Java | 180 + 270 MW | — |
| Lahendong | North Sulawesi | 120 MW | Liquid-dominated |
| Ulubelu | Lampung | 220 MW | — |
| Sarulla | North Sumatra | 330 MW | Largest single contract |

### 7.3 Geological Context

Indonesia's geothermal systems are mostly **volcanic arc systems** associated with subduction:
- Indo-Australian plate subducts beneath Eurasian plate along the Sunda Arc (Sumatra–Java–Bali–Nusa Tenggara)
- Magma generated at ~100 km depth provides heat source for geothermal reservoirs
- Typical reservoir temperatures: 220–300°C at 1.5–3 km depth
- Dominant fluid: meteoric water + small magmatic component

### 7.4 Key Players

- **PT Pertamina Geothermal Energy (PGE)** — state-owned, largest operator
- **Star Energy Geothermal** — operates Salak, Darajat, Wayang Windu
- **PT Geo Dipa Energi** — state-owned, Dieng & Patuha
- **PT PLN** — state electricity company, off-taker of geothermal power
- **Konsultan eksplorasi:** PT GeoACE, Elnusa, Baker Hughes Indonesia

## Key Takeaways

1. **Geothermal energy** is Earth's internal heat, continuously flowing outward — baseload, renewable, and virtually inexhaustible on human timescales.

2. **Two main heat sources:** primordial heat from Earth's formation (~50%) and radiogenic heat from radioactive decay (~50%).

3. **The geothermal gradient** (~25–30°C/km globally, 50–150°C/km in volcanic Indonesia) determines how quickly temperature increases with depth.

4. **Three components needed** for exploitable geothermal energy: heat source + fluid + permeability.

5. **Resource types** range from high-temperature vapor-dominated systems (best for electricity) to low-temperature aquifers (best for direct heating).

6. **Indonesia holds 40% of world geothermal potential** but has developed only ~10% of it — representing enormous career opportunity in exploration and development.

7. **Temperature analysis** is the foundational skill connecting geophysics theory to real-world geothermal exploration and reservoir characterization.
